# Writing Style Guide

Reference for all book content written under this skill.

---

## Voice & Tone

- **Warm and conversational** — like a trusted friend who happens to know the research
- **Never academic** — no jargon, no "it is important to note," no "firstly/secondly/finally"
- **Grade 7 reading level** — short sentences, common words, direct meaning
- **Specific over general** — always give concrete examples, named scenarios, numbers
- **7-10 words per sentence on average** — vary length to create rhythm
- **Active voice** — "She decided" not "It was decided by her"

---

## What NOT to Do

❌ "First and foremost, it is important to note that..."
❌ "Many experts believe that..."
❌ "This chapter will explore..."
❌ "In conclusion, to summarize briefly..."
❌ "Studies have shown that..." (without linking to actual evidence)
❌ " readers should consider..."
❌ Starting every section with "When it comes to..."
❌ Using "however," "therefore," "furthermore" as sentence starters

---

## Chapter Structure

Every chapter follows this pattern:

### 1. Opening Hook (1-2 paragraphs)
Start with a relatable scenario, a surprising fact, or a direct question to the reader. Do NOT begin with a definition, a statistic, or a generic statement. Grab them immediately.

**Good:** "You wake up at 3 a.m. again. Your heart is racing. You convinced yourself last night that you were fine. You were wrong."

**Bad:** "Sleep is essential for human health. Many people struggle with sleep disorders."

### 2. Body (with ## sections and ### subsections)
- Each `##` section = one core idea
- Use `###` subsections only when the idea needs decomposition
- Keep paragraphs short (3-5 sentences max)
- Use concrete examples, reader scenarios, or brief case stories
- Include the occasional rhetorical question to the reader

### 3. Key Takeaway Blockquote
```
> **Key Takeaway:** One clear, memorable sentence that IS the chapter.
```
This is the one thing the reader should remember.

### 4. Chapter Summary (2-3 sentences)
Just recap — no new information.

### 5. What to Do This Week (3-5 action items)
Specific, doable, time-bound actions. Not "take care of yourself" — that's meaningless. Instead: "Cancel one non-essential commitment this week" or "Set a bedtime reminder on your phone for 10 p.m."

---

## Formatting Within Chapters

| Element | Markdown | Usage |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Section heading | `## Section Title` | One per core idea |
| Sub-heading | `### Sub-title` | Optional, when needed |
| Blockquote / Key Takeaway | `> **Key Takeaway:** ...` | End of each chapter |
| Bold | `**text**` | Emphasize key terms |
| List | `- Item` or `1. Item` | Max 5 items, specific not vague |
| Chapter Summary | `**Chapter Summary:**` | Plain text, 2-3 sentences |
| Action items | `**What to do this week:**` | Numbered list |

---

## Voice Consistency Checklist

Before finalizing a chapter, check:
- [ ] Does the opening hook make someone want to keep reading?
- [ ] Did I use any academic-sounding filler phrases?
- [ ] Are all my examples specific (names, numbers, situations) not vague?
- [ ] Is the Key Takeaway actually one clear sentence?
- [ ] Are the action items something a tired, busy person could actually do this week?
- [ ] Did I use any "firstly/secondly/finally" or "in conclusion"?
- [ ] Is the reading level appropriate for Grade 7?
- [ ] Did I avoid emojis in the text? (OK in titles if the agent platform supports them)

## Word Count Targets

| Element | Target |
|---------|--------|
| Chapter (main content) | 800-1,200 words |
| Appendix / Resource list | 600-800 words |
| Chapter Summary | 30-50 words |
| Key Takeaway | 15-25 words |
| Opening Hook | 60-120 words |
| Each action item | 10-25 words |

## Handling Sensitive Topics

For books on health, mental health, burnout, chronic illness, etc.:

- Always include a disclaimer (can be in the book's front matter or back cover)
- Present evidence-based information, not opinion
- Never promise cure outcomes
- Acknowledge that what works varies person to person
- Use "may" and "can" rather than absolute claims ("will," "will cure," "guaranteed")
